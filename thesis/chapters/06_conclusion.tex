\chapter{CONCLUSION}

\section{Summary of Contributions}

This thesis designed and implemented an end-to-end automated competitive intelligence
pipeline for the Amazon consumer electronics marketplace. The key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Data Engineering Framework:} A production-grade pipeline collecting
  daily category rankings, product snapshots, and customer reviews for 3 product
  categories and 30 watchlist ASINs, with zero-downtime automated scheduling via
  GitHub Actions.

  \item \textbf{Quantitative Competitive Metrics:} Two novel composite scores — the
  Listing Quality Score (LQS) and Brand Momentum Score (BMS) — provide standardized
  benchmarks for product competitiveness that practitioners can track longitudinally.

  \item \textbf{Price Segmentation:} K-Means clustering reveals natural price tier
  structures within competitive categories, enabling tier-specific strategic analysis.

  \item \textbf{NLP-Based Sentiment Pipeline:} A RoBERTa inference pipeline produces
  per-ASIN sentiment scores and aspect-level breakdowns from customer reviews,
  providing a leading indicator of rating trajectory.

  \item \textbf{Operational Alerting:} A rule-based alert engine translates metric
  changes into actionable notifications, enabling competitive response without
  manual monitoring.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Observation window:} At 15 days, the data collection period is
  insufficient for robust time series analysis or seasonal pattern detection.
  Findings should be considered illustrative rather than statistically conclusive.

  \item \textbf{Category scope:} Three product categories may not be representative
  of Amazon's broader marketplace dynamics. Categories with different price structures
  (e.g., luxury goods, consumables) may exhibit different competitive patterns.

  \item \textbf{Data completeness:} Approximately [X]\% of watchlist ASINs had
  incomplete daily coverage due to actor resolution mismatches. Complete panel data
  would strengthen the longitudinal analyses.

  \item \textbf{Sentiment model domain:} The RoBERTa model was fine-tuned on social
  media text rather than product reviews. Domain-specific fine-tuning on Amazon review
  data would likely improve classification accuracy.

  \item \textbf{Causal inference:} All analyses in this thesis are correlational.
  Claims about the relationship between listing quality, sentiment, and sales
  performance cannot be interpreted causally without controlled experiments.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Future Work}

Several directions would extend this research:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Extended data collection:} Running the pipeline for 3–6 months would
  enable meaningful seasonality analysis (Prime Day effects, holiday periods) and
  improve forecasting accuracy.

  \item \textbf{Domain-specific sentiment model:} Fine-tuning RoBERTa on Amazon
  product review data across electronics categories would improve aspect-level
  sentiment accuracy.

  \item \textbf{Advertising integration:} Incorporating sponsored placement data
  (Share of Voice) with organic ranking data would enable cross-channel competitive
  analysis.

  \item \textbf{Cross-marketplace comparison:} Extending the pipeline to additional
  Amazon marketplaces (UK, Germany, Japan) would reveal geographic pricing and
  positioning differences.

  \item \textbf{Seller-level analysis:} Tracking third-party seller behavior at the
  ASIN level (Buy Box rotation, fulfillment method) would provide a deeper view of
  marketplace competition.
\end{itemize}
